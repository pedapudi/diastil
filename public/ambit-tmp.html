ambit
where an embedding space loses resolution — and which items.
A walk from first principles to a measure of where a dataset’s neighborhoods are too crowded to tell items apart — and a report that points at them.
the problem · 01

An embedding earns its keep only if things you can tell apart stay apart, and things that belong together stay close.

You embed a dataset; one question decides whether retrieval works: can the space keep its items apart — and where it can’t, which items?

The off-the-shelf answers are single global numbers — mean cosine, IsoScore, effective rank — taken over the whole cloud. But a dataset is rarely uniform. One average can call a space healthy while a sub-population has crammed into a crowded cone, or call it broken when only a couple of cluster directions are stretched. We want a measure that is local, and honest about where geometry stops and the task takes over.

the tension · 02

Two jobs that pull in opposite directions.

recall
Related items must sit at higher cosine than unrelated ones, so nearest-neighbor search actually finds them.
resolution / precision
Unrelated items must sit at low cosine, so you can rank them and impostors don’t crowd the top.

Push related items together for recall and you compress the very angular room you need to rank them apart. The whole game is grouping at the right granularity — tight enough to retrieve a concept together, loose enough that its members stay distinguishable.

the tension · 03

The grouping–resolution budget.

Isotropy is how much angular room is in play; clustering spends it to buy recall; resolution is the room left to keep items apart (precision). “More isotropic” is not the goal — a perfectly uniform space has no grouping at all. Good geometry is structured: clusters spread over the sphere that stay internally roomy.
the goal · 04

Good geometry is daylight.

the stage · 05

Normalize, and an item is a direction.

L2-normalize and an embedding is a point on the unit sphere; cosine similarity is the cosine of the angle between two directions. Length is gone — only orientation remains.

isotropic
Directions spread over the whole sphere. Two random items are almost perpendicular (cos ≈ 0). Lots of room; high resolution.
anisotropic — the “cone”
Directions pile into a narrow patch. Even unrelated items share a high cosine. No room; low resolution.
the stage · 06

In 768 dimensions, “random” means “orthogonal.”

\( \operatorname{sd}(\cos) = 1/\sqrt{d} = 0.036 \) for \(d=768\)

Fix one item. Almost all of a high-dimensional sphere’s area lies in a thin band 90° away from it, so a random direction is almost surely near-orthogonal (cos ≈ 0). The random-pair cosine collapses to a needle-thin spike at 0 — concentration of measure.

Three consequences: “similar” is a tiny window (cos = 0.3 is already 8σ out); a real cluster must be very tight to exist at all; and the average is owned by the diffuse background — the seed of every global-number failure.

what breaks · 07

The pathology you’ve read about: the cone effect.

Representation degeneration (Gao 2019; Ethayarajh 2019): during training the whole cloud drifts toward one cone, so cosine becomes nearly useless — everything is similar to everything.

That is the standard story, and the standard fixes (mean-centering, all-but-the-top, whitening) target it. But it pictures the dataset as a single uniform cloud. Real datasets are not.

what breaks · 08

Crowding is almost always local.

A real dataset is a mix of roomy and crowded regions. Because almost every pair is a near-orthogonal background pair, a mean over pairs — or a covariance summed over points — is dominated by the diffuse majority. A small, intensely crowded pocket barely moves it.

So one global number can call the space healthy while a sub-population is unusable — and it cannot tell you where. Our instruments are global; the problem is local.

the trap · 09

Why a global number lies — the dandelion.

Picture a dandelion: dozens of thin cones radiating from the center. Each cone is internally crowded — its members nearly collinear, unusable — yet the cones point everywhere. Average over all pairs and the within-cone crowding cancels against the between-cone spread: the cloud as a whole looks isotropic, the global number reports “healthy,” and every cone is broken where the scalar cannot see.

the trap · 10

A Simpson’s paradox of isotropy.

Isotropy is a non-collapsible functional of the covariance: the global score is not any weighted average of the per-cluster scores, and it can flip in either direction. “Is this space anisotropic?” has no answer until you say at what stratification. The lesson is Simpson’s: never trust a marginal; condition on the grouping first.

One honest caveat. The dandelion is globally isotropic only when the cones are numerous (more than the dimension). With few clusters, \(\Sigma_{\text{between}}\) has rank \(\le K{-}1\), so the global covariance already shows a low effective rank \(\approx K\) — it is non-specific (can’t say which or where), not blind.
the idea · 11

So ask every point: how tight is your neighborhood?

The shape of the field is the answer.

the method · 14

Calibrate against the reference — don’t eyeball a valley.

Why this is sound. The cutoff is keyed to denser than uniform, so a small but cleanly separated pocket is flagged however short its bump — and a uniformly dense space yields none, its tail simply matching the reference’s. The threshold recalibrates itself at each scale.
the report · 15

What you get.

The field histogram
The distribution of local density against the uniform reference — roomy, globally dense, or pocketed — with the global gap and any pockets marked.
The crowding cloud
The projection recolored and reshaped by each item’s density (dots → pyramids), drag-to-rotate, with a kNN-edge overlay. Read it from color and shape, not position.
Honest about its limits. The field is a k-NN density estimate in cosine units — a rank, not a calibrated density (the cosine→density map is nonlinear in high d), and high-dimensional density estimation suffers distance concentration, hubness, and fixed-k edge bias. Read it as a relative density map and a calibrated pocket detector.
in one breath · 16

ambit.

Embeddings are directions on a sphere; crowding is a region where items get squeezed together; it is almost always local.

So measure crowding as the local density of each item’s neighborhood, calibrate it against a uniform reference, and report the distribution of that field: its modes, the mass in them, and where they are. That describes the anisotropy in a way that a single number cannot.
