Study 05 — the light stage · 04 × 03 hybrid

this mockup is live: click a slide (or the stage/table toggle) to lift; esc drops back
The grammar. One surface, two altitudes. The table is the resting state: slides in flow at reading size, gutter metadata, in-place text edits, the copilot as a rail with cross-slide context. Click a slide (or press ⏎ on a selection) and it lifts to the stage: the slide takes the viewport, chrome turns transient — contextual toolbar at the selection, write-target toast, copilot collapsed to a strip. Esc drops back to the table at the same scroll position. Double-clicking an object in flow lifts and enters it — the enter-the-object rule from every good canvas editor.
Why the pairing works. The two studies fail in opposite directions: the table can't do deep diagram work, the stage can't see the sequence. Each altitude hands off exactly at its failure point — figure work naturally starts with a double-click (→ stage); finishing a slide naturally asks "how does it sit between its neighbors?" (→ esc). No filmstrip anywhere: the table is the filmstrip. The copilot is one conversation whose context follows altitude: "slides 3–5" down, "slide 4 › figure" up.
What it costs. Everything needs two homes (gutter chips become the meta strip; the rail becomes the ⌘. popover) — a discipline, not just a layout. The lift must be a real 60fps zoom, not this mockup's crossfade, or the "one surface" illusion dies. And the table needs virtualization to hold 60 mounted slides. Risks: the lift's discoverability (mitigated by the hover tag and ⏎), and altitude ping-pong if the handoff points are wrong.
